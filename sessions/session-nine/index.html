AI Safety & Critical Thinking

A short, practical guide for teachers • Grades 1–8 • Build healthy skepticism • 30‑minute read

AI is a brilliant assistant, but sometimes it lies. This guide gives you 3 simple rules, 3 classroom activities, and a handful of prompts to turn your students into fact‑checking detectives.

Start Reading ↓

1 · The 3 Big Rules

Before you do anything else, get these three rules in your head. They are the foundation for every conversation you'll have with students about AI.

Rule 1: AI doesn't "know" — it predicts

Key idea: It's a supercharged autocomplete

AI doesn't have a brain or common sense. It looks at billions of examples and guesses the next most likely word. That's why it can sound so confident while being completely wrong.

Say this to students:
  • "AI is like a smart parrot — it repeats patterns, but it doesn't understand."
  • "Always ask: Does this actually make sense?"

Rule 2: AI copies human mistakes

Key idea: Bias comes from the internet

AI learns from human writing, images, and conversations. If people have stereotypes (gender, race, culture), AI will repeat them. We have to actively tell AI to be fair.

Say this to students:
  • "The internet isn't always fair — AI picks up those unfair patterns."
  • "We can fix it by asking for diverse examples."

Rule 3: If it sounds too perfect, question it

Key idea: Confidence ≠ correctness

AI is great at making fake facts sound real. It will invent sources, dates, and details with perfect grammar. The more polished the answer, the more you need to verify it.

Say this to students:
  • "Fake news often sounds very convincing."
  • "Our job is to be the detective and check the facts."
Poster Idea

"AI is the Assistant. YOU are the Boss." — Bosses always check their assistant's work.

2 · 3 Classroom Activities

Each activity takes about 15 minutes. Pick one, try it, and watch your students become healthy skeptics.

Activity A: Fact or Fiction?

Target: Hallucinations (making things up)

Give students 3 sentences about a topic they know. Two are true (from the textbook), one is a believable lie generated by AI. Ask them to spot the lie and explain how they could check it.

Teacher tip:
  • Use a topic you just taught in class.
  • Ask: "Why did the AI add that fake detail?"
  • Takeaway: AI adds fake details to sound good.

Activity B: The Picture Test

Target: Bias (unfair stereotypes)

Use an AI image generator (like Canva or ChatGPT) to create "a picture of a scientist" and "a picture of a nurse." Show the class. Ask why the AI drew mostly men for scientists and mostly women for nurses.

Teacher tip:
  • Then ask: "How can we fix this?"
  • Rewrite the prompt: "a female scientist" or "a male nurse."
  • Takeaway: We have to tell AI to be fair.

Activity C: The Source Check

Target: Verification habit

Use a tool like Perplexity AI that shows sources. Ask it a question and show the sources at the bottom. Ask students to find the exact sentence in the source that proves the AI is telling the truth.

Teacher tip:
  • If the AI doesn't show sources, treat it like a rumor.
  • Cross-check with a second tool or a book.
  • Takeaway: Always ask "Where did you get that?"

3 · Golden Prompts

Copy-paste these prompts into any AI tool to force it to be honest and transparent. They add "safety rails" to your questions.

Level:

Safety Prompts

Honesty Basic
“Explain [Topic], but if you are not completely sure, just say 'I don't know'.”
Confidence check Basic
“Give me 3 facts about [Topic]. Tell me which one you are least sure about.”
Sources Intermediate
“Summarise [Topic] and list the specific sources you used for each claim.”
Multiple perspectives Intermediate
“Give me two different viewpoints on [Topic] so I don't get a one-sided answer.”
Counterarguments Advanced
“What are the strongest counterarguments to your answer about [Topic]?”
Bias audit Advanced
“Read this draft text [paste] and tell me if it contains any gender, cultural, or racial bias. Suggest a neutral rewrite.”
Pro tip

Add this permanent instruction to your ChatGPT or Gemini settings: "Always tell me if you are guessing or unsure. Cite sources when possible."
